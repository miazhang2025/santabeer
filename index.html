The Idea

An Ad Hidden Inside a Joke
Hidden Inside an Ad.

One pretending to be the other. Both exhausted by the performance.

We were making an ad for a holiday beer — a product that sits at the center of celebration, yet carries awkward cultural baggage: the seasonal guilt of indulgence. People love beer, but it's often framed as something to earn, burn off, or feel guilty about. Instead of avoiding that association, we reversed it.

The concept builds on a familiar but rarely questioned convention in weight-loss advertising: "before and after" transformations are often portrayed by two different actors. We realized the characters from The Nightmare Before Christmas were a perfect fit — the film is literally about Jack Skellington kidnapping Santa Claus and impersonating him. A before-and-after identity swap that maps directly onto fake transformations and stolen roles.

The reveal shifts the narrative from fantasy to reality: two exhausted actors, still in costume, sharing a beer in a parking lot. Beer is no longer about image or discipline — it's about what people actually love about it: sharing, joy, and the freedom to be yourself.

AI in Advertising

AI Deployed in Service
of a Strong Idea.

The Coca-Cola Problem

In 2025, when Coca-Cola used AI to remake its beloved 1995 Holidays Are Coming Christmas ad, the backlash was swift — critics called it soulless, with many noting the irony of the "Real Magic" tagline. What was missing was storytelling: AI-generated imagery snapped together with a familiar jingle, dead on arrival. The lesson isn't that AI doesn't belong in advertising — it's that AI deployed instead of a story produces nothing. AI deployed in service of a strong idea is something else entirely.

Think of how AI has transformed software engineering — not by replacing developers, but by handling scaffolding so they can focus on architecture and judgment. The same shift applies to creative production. Stop-motion is one of advertising's most expensive visual styles, routinely cut from budgets before the conversation even starts. AI changed that equation: by locking the clay stop-motion aesthetic early in ComfyUI and Nano Banana, we could generate consistent, on-style visuals across the full production without physical puppets or sets.

The style we chose wasn't a budget compromise — it was the best fit for the idea, and the most natural home for the dark holiday whimsy we were drawing from. Since The Nightmare Before Christmas is itself a stop-motion classic, the visual world already existed. We were simply moving into it. AI didn't make this project cheaper. It made the right choice possible.

The Process

How We Made It

01
TapNow
Concept & Storyboard

One evening of brainstorming locked in the narrative structure, the meta-twist, and the final tagline. Storyboarding in TapNow let us plan shot compositions and camera logic before committing to any generation — preventing costly detours later.

02
ComfyUINano Banana
Style Lock & Character Design

We established a clay stop-motion aesthetic as a deliberate stylistic choice — handmade and stylised, counteracting the cold irony of the satire. Since The Nightmare Before Christmas is itself a stop-motion classic, the visual world already existed. We were simply moving into it. Locking character references early was essential for consistency across every shot.

03
ClaudeGemini
Prompt Engineering

Claude and Gemini refined shot descriptions, tightened prompt language for each scene, and iterated on the final punchline until it landed without over-explaining. The tagline — Real Taste, No Acting Required — went through multiple passes before it felt inevitable.

04
Nano BananaKlingRunway Gen-4
Keyframe-First Video Generation

Rather than prompting video cold, we generated key frames in Nano Banana first, then iterated to produce refined intermediate frames. These served as anchors for Kling and Runway, giving us significantly more control over character continuity and motion than starting from text alone.

05
ElevenLabsPremiere Pro
Audio & Final Edit

A rough cut was assembled first, then ElevenLabs generated character voices and key ambient audio against that edit. Final integration and polish happened in Premiere Pro — with the rough cut informing every audio decision rather than the other way around.

The Hard Part

When Santa Gets Stuck,
So Does the Prompt.

Problem
The Chimney Shot

The cross-section shot of Santa wedged in the chimney — the visual anchor of the entire ad's premise — refused to generate cleanly. Every prompt combination produced something wrong: wrong angle, wrong proportions, missing the black comedy of the situation.

✕

Attempted direct text-to-video prompting across multiple iterations — no prompt produced a usable cross-section view.

→

Built a precise reference illustration in Photoshop — a hybrid human-AI workflow, drawing the exact geometry and proportions manually.

✓

Used the Photoshop illustration as a generation anchor. It became one of the most visually distinctive frames in the piece.

The Takeaway

Refusing to Compromise.

The best argument for AI in advertising isn't efficiency — it's creative possibility. A strong idea came first, and AI served it at every stage: not by replacing creative judgment, but by removing the constraints that would have forced us to compromise it. We got the visual style the concept deserved, the production quality a spec project couldn't otherwise afford, and the freedom to follow the idea wherever it needed to go. The stop-motion world, the character consistency, the hybrid human-AI problem-solving — none of it was about cutting corners. It was about refusing to.
